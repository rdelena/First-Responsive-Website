Art Gallery & Art History

Impressionism (c. 1867-1886)-

In the late 19th century France, an art movement called Impressionism began to take foot in the art community, contrary to the rules of academic painting during that time. Impressionism paintings are characterized by their use of small, thin, visible brush strokes, capturing everyday moments, use of colors & tones without mixing the paint, and an absence of traditional drawings. It represents the affirmation of the freedom of individual expression of the artist.

Post-Impressionism (c. 1886-1905)

The Post-Impressionism art movement was formed in opposition of the Impressionist stylistic use of naturalistic depiction of light and color, and the loss of structure in paintings. Post-Impressionism characteristics include the use of vivid colors, thick application of paint, distinctive brush strokes, and real-life subject matter, but they were more inclined to emphasize geometric forms, to distort form for expressive effect, and to use unnatural or arbitrary color.

Fauvism (c. 1905-1910)

The Fauvism movement was created by a group of modern artists with an emphasis on using strong color over representation, or realistic values, of Impressionism. Fauvism characteristics included the use of bold, vibrant colors used in unusual juxtaposition, and high applications of paint. Fauvist painters use recognizable imagery with chromatic dissonance to allow the color to convey meaning in itself without the outside connection to the depicted object.
